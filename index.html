How to change name in Maine

About this posting

This posting will talk about my last project for a class I am taking now as a part of my Legal Technology and Innovation concentration at my school. The class is called "Lawyering in the Age of Smart Machines". The class exposed me into the current legal technologies and trend such as QnA, Hotdocs, and A2J Author, and taught me how the legal practice could be efficient and access to justice could be easier utilizing legal technology. QnA is a Markup language that is easy enough that people with little or no programming experience. It can transform blocks of text into interactive question and answer sessions (QnAs). It was easy enough that within minutes, we were able to build few basic question and answer sessions. Hotdocs is a document automation software hat enables the transformation of word-processing documents and graphical (PDF) forms into document-generation templates. A2J Author is a software tool that delivers greater access to justice for self-represented litigants by enabling non-technical legal service professionals to easily build and implement user-friendly web-based interfaces for document assembly. My Project - How to change name in Maine Life involves changes. Sometimes you might need to change your name because you are getting married, divorced, or for other reasons. In Maine, a person must go to their local probate court in the county in which they reside to petition the court for a name change. Whether you're changing your name because of marriage or divorce, or changing the name of a minor, you will need to contact your county's probate court. Pine Tree Legal Assistance ("PTLA") has a great website with important information posted already about how to change name in Maine. PTLA has may legal forms involving Family Law with Hotdocs for user friendly court form generation but was missing court forms for petition to change name. Utilizing QnA, Hotdocs, and A2J Author, I have created an interactive user-friendly web-based interface that will help people to get information and create court forms to use. One tricky part for the Maine probate court was that they require specific type of paper for the forms filed with the court ( "20 lbs. acid-free natural-white 25% cotton fiber paper [using] 600 dot per inch black laser printer or better."). So, self-printed forms might risk the probate register refusing to file the forms. I instructed my users to print out the form and use the information to fill in the form bought in court.
